DETECTING PROPAGANDA
A propaganda is a persuasive idea or statement with biased argument(s). It does not give solid evidence to support a point but instead uses emotional appeal. It is often used in campaigns, advertisements, and litical speeches to influence or sway the opinion of the audience. Sometimes, a propaganda is so appealing that it does not leave room for people to analyze the appeal. But when you familiarize yourself with the types of propaganda, it will help you recognize one easily.
  1. Testimonial - the use of popular people, to advertise or claim that something is effective even if they do nor have the expertise on the field. For example, a famous celebrity giving his or her testimony that he or she is using a certain skin whitening product.
  2. Glittering generalities - the use of words that create positive reactions to mind of the viewers or listeners. For example, an advertisement for a food supplement claiming that it is 100 percent nature and organic.
  3. Transfer - the technique of carrying over the prestige and authority of a symbol or a concept something or someone. An example of which is a medicine advertisement claiming that it is the brand most prescribed by doctors.
  4. Plain folks the use of everyday situations or activities to appeal to the masses or to show that this to brand most prescribed by doctors The person or idea is one with the majority. For example, a politician she MRT wants to present himself or herself as an ordinary people whom everyone can relate to.
  5. Bandwagon - persuading people to go with what everyone else is doing the people's desire to be at the "winning side" or the majority. For example, an advertisement for a deal to detergent brand claiming that 9 out of 10 mothers use it.
  6. Name calling- using brands or names to evoke negative perception to the idea or person involved. This aims to induce hatred to t being called. This includes calling a politician pig, an artist as a bum, or any other person as a scum.
  7. Card stacking - strategy of telling half-truths about something by highlighting the best features. For example, a milk product claiming that it is low fat omit the fact that it is high in calorie content.

LITERARY DEVICES IN 18TH CENTURY SERMONS
A poem, a novel, or a play might easily be considered as literature by anyone as these are the common literary forms in contemporary times. The sermon' s literary qualities, on the other hand, might not be very evident but they also make use of different literary techniques such as figures of speech which give it it’s literary quality. Thus, similar to any literary text, 18th century sermons may also be analyze for its imagery, symbolism and figures of speech.
"Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God" is an example of an 18th century sermon that is rich in figurative language. The author's use of such device is careful, clear, and meaningful. Study some of the literary devices used in the text:
  • Imagery is creating mental pictures that appeal to the five senses of the reader.
    • Visual (appeals to the sense of sight): "They stand waiting for them, like greedy hungry Lion that see their Prey, and expect to have it, but are for the present kept back"
    • Olfactory (appeals to the sense of smell): "the flesh burnt in the pit of hell”
    • Gustatory (appeals to the sense of taste): "one can have a rational sense of the sweetness of honey, but the true sense of its taste can only be attained through experience.
    • Auditory (appeals to the sense of hearing)."but when once Day of Mercy is past, your most with lamentable and dolorous Cries and Shrieks will vain”
    • Tactile (appeals to the sense of touch)"Fire is made ready, the Furnace is now hot”
    • Kinesthetic (appeals to the sense of motion or movement) “as one that stands or walks in slippery Places is always exposed to fall”
  • Symbolism is the use of images (object or actions) that represent something else. Often, the symbols used possess universal or cultural value or meaning.
    • Examples:
    • The cross is a symbol of religion.
    • The snake is a symbol of temptation
    • The dragon is symbol of threat for the West but it symbolizes protection and luck for the East.
  • Figurative of speech are words or phrases that are used to create a specialized meaning that is not based on the literal meaning of the words.
    • Personification (using qualities, capabilities, and features of a person to nonliving thing): “Justice calls aloud for an infinite Punishment of their Sin”
    • Similie (indirect comparison using the word like and as)”: “your Destruction would come like a Whirlwind”
    • Metaphor (direct comparison of two unrelated ideas or things): “God is an archer”
    • Hyperbole (an obvious exaggeration): “Millions die every second because of sins.”
  • Rhetorical devices
    • Alliteration (repetition of initiation consonant sounds): “sweet summer sins”
    • Consonance (Repetition of initial consonant sound in two or more times in all syllables of each word): “priest who tap on people”
    • Assonance (repetition of the same vowel sound in the two or more times in all syllables of each word): “another anti-war party”
    • Anaphora (repetition of words or phrase in succession) (e.g., Come live with me and be my love in the pastoral poem The Passionate Shepherd to His Love)

Figurative language, like the use of imagery, symbolisms, figures of speech, or rhetorical devices, makes the literary text relatable to the readers. Sometimes, the representation of an unfamiliar idea using a common illustration is the key to understanding figurative language does not complicate the text, instead, it cushions understanding by means of comparisons and relations.

GETTING INFORMATION FROM BROCHURES, PAMPHLETS, AND PERIODICALS

Informational materials such as brochures, pamphlets, leaflets, and periodicals provide quick and handy information. Knowing the differences among these materials may help you identify the kind of information that you will expect from each.
  1. A brochure is a reference material that usually has four to six panels. It is intended to be kept for present and future reference. The contents of a brochure are usually factual details of an institution, a business company, or an organization; thus, these are not intended to be replaced often. You can see brochures distributed in schools upon enrollment or placed at the entrance or lobbies of hotels banks, and offices.
  2. A pamphlet is an unbound booklet that has at least 5 but not more than 48 pages, exclusive of cover pages. These pages aim to educate, inform, persuade or entertain, advertise, and campaign.
  3. A periodical is a document with consistent schedule and frequency of publication. Newsletters, journals, gazettes, digests, and magazines are all periodicals. Periodicals contain updated information that may help the distributor to achieve his or her goals. For example, companies that regularly distribute newsletters give you the impression that their business is doing well and is actively finding means to serve you better.
  4. A flyer is a piece of paper that is intended for easy information handling. This is used to provide the gist of an event, organization or concept, with the assumption that the people who will read it will ask further information. They are way" such that, after one reads the information, it can be discarded. Promotions like events, product information, or conferences are usually delivered by flyers.
  5. A leaflet is just like a flyer, but is printed out on a bigger paper or printed in color and aims to catch longer attention of the target audience. Leaflets may be inserted in local newspapers or may just be left in places where the target audience will most likely see.

ZERO AND FIRST DEGREE CONDITIONALS

Conditional sentences sentences express actions that can only take place if a certain condition is fulfilled. these sentences are also called "if clauses" because they are always made up of an if clause (dependent clause )and a main clause (independent clause).
Example:
if clause (condition)                           main clause (result)
If I read the brochures, I become informed.

There are different types of conditional: Zero degree conditional, first degree conditional, second degree conditional, and third degree conditional. This section focuses on the first two type of conditionals.
Zero Degree Conditional. The zero degree conditional is used when the situation is real and possible. It is often used to refer to general truths and facts, thus, uses the simple present tense in both parts of the sentence.
Examples:
  1. If you study hard, you get good grades.
  2. If it rains, the grass gets wet.
  3. If the people do not read news, they remain uninformed.

First Defree Conditional. This type of condition is used to refers to a possible condition its probable result. It uses the simple present in the if clause and the simple future in the main clause.
  1. If there are no classes, we will go to the park.
  2. If you receive the flyer’s voucher, you will have a free ticket.
  3. If you make a mistake at printing you will repeat this.

WRITING A DISCURSIVE PAPER

A discursive paper is a text that presents an objective and balanced explanation of a given topic Although neutral, the paper does not express its neutrality explicitly by stating a writer, I am neutral about the subject because of the following reasons. A discursive paper examines the subject objectively and distances the author from the paper itself. The opinion of the author does not matter much because the goal is not to influence the readers of the author's preference, but to carefully examine two sides of an issue.
Wonder and Wisdom
What makes anyone intelligent is the awareness of what and how to think about the subject he or she deals with.
A Discursive paper is basically comprised of three elements: arguments for the issue, arguments against issue, and balanced treatment of the issue. Similar to any other type of essay, it should also have a good introduction, body and conclusion.
  • A good introduction aside from introduction the topic, gives some background about the topic. Quotations and anecdotes are also helpful to get the attention of the readers.
  • The body must present the arguments both for and against the issue. It is also good to have a balance presentation of both sides of the argument.
  • A good conclusion should contain no new information; although, it may contain the personal opinion of the writer on the issue.
  • Additionally, remember that effective discursive essays must be balanced, organized, and clear.
    Take note of the three C's in writing a discursive paper.
  • Correct. You have to make sure that the information you provide are correct. Research on the things that you want to discuss before actually writing them. If you aim to actually present a balanced paper, the quality of information for both sides must be secured.
  • Concise. A lengthy paper does not mean a meaty one. Being concise helps your readers understand the point of your paper with lesser chances of misrepresenting data. Conciseness also shows how well versed you are in expressing yourself.
  • Clear. What is the use of a well-researched paper expressed at a reasonable length if the meaning is not clear?' Clarity is important so that the readers will understand your point(s). You can achieve this by using appropriate diction, correct transition, and adequate examples in the discussion.
